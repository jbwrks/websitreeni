Your MSME Suppliers Are Drowning in ESG Questionnaires

And if you're a large enterprise, it's your problem too. Here's why—and what you can do about it.

Rajesh runs a mid-sized precision components manufacturer in Pune. Fifty employees. ₹40 crore revenue. Supplies to three large automotive OEMs. In the last six months, he's received 11 different ESG questionnaires. Each one is 30-50 pages. Each customer wants different data. Each has a different deadline.

Rajesh doesn't have a sustainability team. He has a GM who's juggling production, quality, and now ESG reporting. The GM spent 80 hours in Q4 2025 just filling out forms. Most of it was guesswork—they don't track half the metrics customers are asking for.

And Rajesh is one of the lucky ones. He can afford to spend the time. Smaller suppliers are just ignoring the questionnaires.

Why MSMEs Are Struggling

Large enterprises have sustainability teams, consultants, and software platforms. MSMEs have none of that. Yet they're being asked to report the same metrics:

  • Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions (most don't know what that means)
  • Water consumption and discharge (they pay a flat water bill, don't have meters)
  • Waste segregation and recycling rates (they have one bin)
  • Labor practices, safety incidents, training hours (tracked on paper, if at all)
  • Governance policies (they have an owner and a GM, that's the governance structure)

And every customer wants this data in a different format. Some use EcoVadis. Some use CDP Supply Chain. Some send custom Excel templates. There's no standardization.

The Cascade Effect

Why is this happening in 2026? Because ESG compliance is cascading down the value chain.

Your company (a large enterprise) is being asked for Scope 3 emissions by investors, regulators, and customers. You can't report Scope 3 without supplier data. So you send questionnaires to your suppliers.

Your suppliers are MSMEs. They're also suppliers to other large enterprises. So they're getting questionnaires from multiple customers. Each customer wants data urgently (because they have their own reporting deadlines).

The result? Small suppliers are overwhelmed. Response rates are 10-20%. The data you do get is often incomplete or inaccurate.

Why This Is Your Problem

If you're a large enterprise thinking, "Well, that's their problem," consider this:

1. Your Scope 3 data will be garbage. If suppliers are guessing, your emissions calculations are wrong. And starting FY 2025-26, BRSR Core requires value chain disclosures with assurance. Auditors will ask: "How did you verify supplier data?" "We sent them a form" won't fly.

2. You're creating supplier churn. Small suppliers are choosing to work with customers who don't burden them with ESG paperwork. If your ESG program is too demanding, you risk losing good suppliers.

3. You're missing opportunities. Many MSMEs are actually doing good sustainability work—renewable energy, waste reduction, worker welfare—they just don't know how to report it. By not helping them document it, you miss chances to improve your own Scope 3 footprint and recognize sustainable suppliers.

What Good Enterprises Are Doing

The companies getting supplier ESG right are taking a different approach:

1. They segment suppliers by capability.

Not all suppliers can provide the same level of data. Tier 1 suppliers (large, sophisticated) can handle detailed questionnaires. Tier 2 and 3 suppliers (MSMEs) need simpler requests and more support.

Smart enterprises create tiered engagement: detailed data from top 50 suppliers (covering 70% of spend), simplified questionnaires for the next 200 (covering 20% of spend), and estimates for the long tail.

2. They make it easy to respond.

Instead of sending a 40-page Excel template, they use simple online forms with:

  • Guided questions ("Enter your electricity consumption in kWh from your bill")
  • Pre-filled data where possible (procurement system already knows supplier's revenue)
  • Built-in calculators ("Don't know your emissions? We'll calculate it based on your energy data")
  • Mobile-friendly (many MSME owners don't work from laptops)

3. They provide support.

Some enterprises run "supplier sustainability clinics"—webinars, help desks, or even on-site support to help MSMEs understand what's being asked and how to gather data.

Others offer incentives: faster payment terms for suppliers who provide ESG data, public recognition programs, or co-investment in sustainability improvements.

4. They collaborate with peers.

If three automotive OEMs all buy from the same 100 component suppliers, why send three separate questionnaires? Industry consortia are emerging where companies agree to accept shared ESG assessments.

This reduces supplier burden and improves data quality (suppliers have more time to get one questionnaire right than to rush through three).

A Case Study: What We Learned

We worked with a large IT services company that wanted Scope 3 data from 500 suppliers—mostly small Indian firms providing facilities management, catering, and local transport services.

Initial approach: Sent them a 30-page CDP-style questionnaire. Response rate: 12%.

Revised approach:

  • Created a simple 10-question online form (electricity, diesel, LPG, water, waste—basic operational data)
  • Provided a video tutorial in Hindi and English
  • Offered a help desk (phone and WhatsApp support)
  • Extended the deadline and sent personalized reminders

Response rate jumped to 68%. Data quality improved because suppliers understood what was being asked. The client got credible Scope 3 data covering 80% of their spend.

The Bottom Line

MSMEs are the backbone of India's economy—and your supply chain. They can't be expected to have the same ESG capabilities as Fortune 500 companies. But with the right approach, they can provide meaningful data.

The question isn't whether to engage MSMEs on ESG. With BRSR Core value chain requirements, you don't have a choice. The question is: will you make it easy for them, or will you overwhelm them and get garbage data?

In 2026, supplier sustainability programs that work are built on empathy, simplicity, and support—not just compliance demands.
