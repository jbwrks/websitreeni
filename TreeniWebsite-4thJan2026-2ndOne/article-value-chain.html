Value Chain ESG Reporting: What SEBI's New Rules Mean

Starting FY 2025-26, you need to report on partners who account for 2% or more of purchases/sales. Here's what that actually requires.

Buried in SEBI's December 2024 BRSR Core amendments is a requirement most companies haven't noticed yet: mandatory value chain ESG disclosures. If your upstream or downstream partners account for 2% or more of your total purchases or sales, you must report their ESG performance.

This isn't just about carbon emissions. It's governance, labor practices, water usage, waste management—the whole ESG spectrum. And it starts in FY 2025-26, which means you have less than a year to prepare.

What Does "Value Chain Partners" Mean?

Upstream: Suppliers providing goods and services. If a supplier accounts for 2% or more of your total procurement spend, you need their ESG data.

Downstream: Customers buying your products/services. If a customer accounts for 2% or more of your total revenue, you need their ESG data too.

Example: An auto component manufacturer has:

  • Total purchases: ₹500 crore
  • Supplier A (steel): ₹15 crore (3% of purchases) → Must report
  • Supplier B (packaging): ₹8 crore (1.6%) → Not required
  • Total revenue: ₹800 crore
  • Customer X (auto OEM): ₹120 crore (15% of sales) → Must report

The 2% threshold sounds small. It's not. For most companies, it covers 20-40 entities.

What ESG Data Do You Need?

SEBI hasn't specified exact metrics yet (detailed guidelines expected Q1 2026), but based on BRSR Core structure, expect:

Environmental:

  • Scope 1 & 2 emissions (absolute and intensity)
  • Water consumption and discharge
  • Waste generation and recycling rates
  • Air pollutant emissions (NOx, SOx, PM)

Social:

  • Employee diversity (gender, differently-abled)
  • Safety incidents (LTIFR - Lost Time Injury Frequency Rate)
  • Training hours per employee
  • Living wage compliance

Governance:

  • Anti-corruption training coverage
  • Board independence percentage
  • Data privacy policies

You're not just asking "What's your carbon footprint?" You're conducting full ESG due diligence on key value chain partners.

Why This Is Harder Than It Sounds

Challenge 1: Supplier willingness.

You can ask your suppliers for data. They can say no. Unlike financial audits, you don't have legal rights to ESG data (unless it's in your procurement contract, which it probably isn't).

Large, sophisticated suppliers may cooperate (they're facing similar requirements from their own stakeholders). Small suppliers may ignore the request or provide incomplete data.

Challenge 2: Customer data.

Asking customers for their ESG data is awkward. You're essentially saying, "To comply with our reporting, we need to audit you." Large customers (especially if they're multinationals) may share publicly available sustainability reports. But what if your major customer is a private company with no ESG reporting?

Challenge 3: Data inconsistency.

Even if partners cooperate, they may:

  • Use different methodologies (location-based vs market-based emissions)
  • Report for different time periods (fiscal vs calendar year)
  • Have different scope boundaries (what they consider "operational control")
  • Provide unverified data (no third-party assurance)

Consolidating all this into your BRSR report requires normalization and judgment calls.

What You Should Do Now

Step 1: Identify who's covered.

Pull your procurement and sales data. Identify entities accounting for 2% or more. You might have 15-50 entities depending on concentration.

Step 2: Assess their ESG reporting capability.

Do they already publish sustainability reports? Are they BRSR-compliant themselves? Or are they MSMEs with no ESG infrastructure?

This determines your engagement approach:

  • Sophisticated partners: Request existing reports, align on methodology
  • Mid-capability partners: Provide guided questionnaires
  • Low-capability partners: Offer support or use proxy data with disclaimers

Step 3: Update procurement contracts.

Going forward, your RFPs and supplier agreements should include ESG data sharing clauses. Make it a contractual obligation, not a request. Companies that started this in 2024-25 will have compliant data by 2026. Companies starting now will struggle.

Step 4: Build a value chain ESG platform.

You can't do this in Excel. You need a system that:

  • Tracks which partners are covered by the 2% threshold
  • Sends data requests with guided templates
  • Collects and validates responses
  • Flags missing or anomalous data
  • Generates consolidated disclosures for BRSR

Step 5: Prepare for assurance challenges.

Value chain data will be part of your BRSR Core assurance. Auditors will ask: "How did you verify this data? What if the supplier's numbers are wrong?"

You need a defensible process:

  • Document data sources and methodologies
  • Conduct reasonableness checks (compare to industry benchmarks)
  • For high-risk partners, request third-party verification
  • Disclose limitations ("This data is self-reported by partners without independent assurance")

The Strategic Opportunity

Yes, this is compliance-driven. But it's also a strategic opportunity.

Supply chain risk management. ESG data reveals operational risks (high water usage in drought-prone areas, poor safety records, governance red flags). You can use this for supplier risk scoring and strategic sourcing decisions.

Collaborative decarbonization. Once you have supplier emissions data, you can identify hotspots and work with suppliers on reduction programs. This improves your Scope 3 footprint and strengthens supplier relationships.

Competitive advantage. Companies that build robust value chain ESG programs in 2026 will be ahead of peers. When customers or investors ask for supply chain transparency, you'll have data. Your competitors will be scrambling.

The Bottom Line

Value chain ESG reporting isn't optional. It's mandated by SEBI starting FY 2025-26. The companies treating this as a one-time compliance exercise will produce low-quality disclosures and face assurance challenges.

The companies building structured programs—clear identification of covered partners, systematic data collection, contractual frameworks, and validation processes—will turn compliance into competitive advantage. They'll have visibility into value chain risks and opportunities that their peers lack.
