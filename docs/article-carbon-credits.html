Carbon Credits Are Not a Climate Strategy

Unpopular opinion: If your Net Zero plan relies heavily on offsets, you don't have a plan. You have an accounting trick.

In January 2026, a major Indian conglomerate announced they're "carbon neutral." How? They bought carbon credits equivalent to their annual emissions. No operational changes. No energy transition. No supplier engagement. Just ₹15 crore worth of forestry offsets. Press release done.

This is becoming common. And it's a problem.

What Are Carbon Credits?

A carbon credit represents one tonne of CO2 either removed from the atmosphere or avoided. Companies can buy these credits to "offset" their own emissions.

Common types:

  • Reforestation projects: Planting trees that absorb CO2
  • Renewable energy projects: Wind/solar farms that displace fossil fuel generation
  • Methane capture: Capturing landfill gas or agricultural methane
  • Cookstove distribution: Efficient cookstoves that reduce wood burning

In theory, if you emit 10,000 tonnes and buy 10,000 credits, you're "net zero." The atmosphere doesn't care where emissions are reduced, right?

Wrong. Here's why.

Problem 1: Additionality Is Hard to Prove

For a carbon credit to be legitimate, the emissions reduction must be additional—it wouldn't have happened without the carbon finance.

Example: A wind farm in Tamil Nadu generates carbon credits. But the project was economically viable without carbon revenue (electricity sales + government subsidies already made it profitable). Is it additional? Debatable.

Many carbon credit projects would have happened anyway. The credits are just extra revenue. Buying them doesn't create new climate action—it finances activity that was already going to occur.

Problem 2: Permanence Is Uncertain

Tree-planting projects are popular. But trees can die, burn in wildfires, or be cut down later. When that happens, the stored carbon is released back into the atmosphere.

Your emissions from burning diesel? Permanent. The "offset" from a forest? Maybe permanent, maybe not. That's not equivalence.

In 2025, massive wildfires in Greece and California burned forests that were part of carbon offset programs. Millions of tonnes of "offset" emissions were released in days. The companies that bought those credits? Still claiming carbon neutrality.

Problem 3: Verification Is Weak

Carbon credit registries (Verra, Gold Standard, etc.) have standards. But enforcement is inconsistent. Investigative reports in 2024-2025 found:

  • Forestry projects claiming credit for trees that were already protected
  • Renewable energy projects double-counting (selling credits to multiple buyers)
  • Baseline manipulation (claiming avoided emissions that were never going to happen)

Are all credits fraudulent? No. But the market is unregulated enough that buying low-quality credits is easy—and cheap.

Problem 4: It Delays Real Action

This is the biggest issue. Carbon credits create a moral hazard.

If a company can buy neutrality for ₹15 crore instead of spending ₹200 crore on energy efficiency, renewable power, and process improvements, what do you think they'll do?

Offsets become a substitute for decarbonization. Companies claim "net zero" while their actual emissions keep rising. They're not solving the problem—they're paying someone else to claim they solved it.

What Science-Based Targets Say

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)—the gold standard for corporate climate action—is clear: offsets don't count toward your emissions reduction target.

If you commit to reducing emissions 50% by 2030, you must achieve that through operational changes. You can't buy your way there with offsets.

SBTi allows offsets only for residual emissions—the 5-10% you genuinely can't eliminate (e.g., aviation emissions, certain industrial processes). Everything else must be real reductions.

When Are Carbon Credits Acceptable?

Not all use of carbon credits is greenwashing. Legitimate use cases exist:

1. Neutralizing unavoidable emissions. Airlines can't fully decarbonize by 2030 (SAF is expensive and scarce, electric planes are decades away). High-quality offsets for aviation emissions are defensible—if coupled with aggressive efforts to reduce flying and maximize efficiency.

2. Going beyond Net Zero. If you've already reduced emissions 90% through operational changes and want to offset the remaining 10% plus fund additional climate action, that's credible. You've done the hard work first.

3. Supporting high-integrity projects. Not all offsets are equal. Buying credits from verified, high-permanence projects (direct air capture, enhanced weathering, biochar) is better than buying cheap forestry credits of dubious quality.

The key: offsets should be a last resort, not your primary strategy.

What a Real Decarbonization Strategy Looks Like

Companies serious about Net Zero are doing this:

  1. Measure accurately. Get baseline emissions right. Include all Scope 3 categories.
  2. Set science-based targets. Not "net zero by 2050" (too vague). Specific near-term targets: 50% reduction by 2030, validated by SBTi.
  3. Reduce Scope 1 & 2 first. Switch to renewables, improve energy efficiency, electrify processes, optimize logistics.
  4. Engage the value chain. Work with suppliers on reduction programs. Shift procurement to low-carbon alternatives.
  5. Invest in innovation. Support R&D for hard-to-abate sectors. Fund pilot programs for emerging technologies.
  6. Use offsets only for residuals. After exhausting reduction opportunities, offset what you genuinely can't eliminate with high-quality credits.

Notice the order? Offsets are step 6, not step 1.

The Investor Perspective

Sophisticated investors see through offset-heavy Net Zero claims. They're asking:

  • What's your absolute emissions trend? (Not net, actual.)
  • What percentage of your "net zero" claim is offsets vs reductions?
  • Do you have SBTi-validated targets?
  • What's your CapEx allocation for decarbonization?

Companies that say "we're carbon neutral" but show no operational changes get flagged as high ESG risk.

The Bottom Line

Carbon credits aren't inherently bad. They can fund genuine climate projects. But they're not a substitute for reducing your own emissions.

If your Net Zero strategy is "keep emitting, buy offsets," you're not solving climate change. You're greenwashing.

In 2026, with BRSR Core disclosures and increasing scrutiny, that approach won't work. Stakeholders—investors, customers, regulators—are demanding real reductions. The companies that focused on actual decarbonization will thrive. The ones that relied on cheap offsets will be exposed.
