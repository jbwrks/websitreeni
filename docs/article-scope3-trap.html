The Scope 3 Trap: Why Your Emissions Are Probably 10X Higher Than You Think

The uncomfortable truth about spend-based carbon accounting and why it's time to stop lying to yourself

Let me guess: You calculated your Scope 3 emissions using spend-based factors. Your report says your supply chain emissions are modest—maybe 30-40% of your total footprint. You presented it to the board. Everyone nodded. Job done. Except... you're probably wrong. Way wrong.

The Spend-Based Illusion

Here's how most companies calculate Scope 3 emissions today:

  1. Take your procurement spend by category (e.g., ₹50 crore on steel)
  2. Multiply by a generic emission factor (e.g., 1.8 kg CO2e per ₹1000 spent on steel)
  3. Sum it all up. Done.

This is called "spend-based" carbon accounting, and it's wildly inaccurate. Why? Because it assumes all steel is the same. All logistics companies operate identically. All suppliers in a category have the same carbon intensity.

In reality:

  • One steel supplier uses renewable energy. Another uses coal-fired blast furnaces. Their emissions per ton differ by 3-5X.
  • One logistics partner optimizes routes and uses electric trucks. Another doesn't. Emissions differ by 50%.
  • Your IT hardware vendor sources from a high-efficiency fab in Taiwan. Your competitor's vendor sources from an old fab in China. Emissions differ by 2X.

Spend-based factors erase all this nuance. You're using an industry average that might be completely irrelevant to your actual suppliers.

Why This Matters in 2026

Two things are changing fast:

1. Regulators are cracking down. SEBI's BRSR Core framework requires companies to report Scope 3 across at least 15 categories. Starting FY 2025-26, value chain ESG disclosures are mandatory for companies whose upstream or downstream partners account for 2% or more of purchases/sales. That means you need supplier-specific data, not generic estimates.

2. Customers are asking for it. If you're a supplier to a European company, you're already getting questionnaires asking for product-level carbon footprints. "We spent ₹X on your product, so we estimated Y emissions" won't fly. They want your actual data: energy consumption, logistics emissions, raw material sources.

The Real Cost of Bad Data

Let's say you're an IT services company. You reported 100,000 tCO2e in total emissions. Using spend-based factors, you estimated Scope 3 at 60,000 tCO2e (60% of total). That sounds reasonable.

Then you actually engaged your suppliers. You collected primary data from your top 200 vendors. Turns out:

  • Your data center provider's emissions are 2X higher than the industry average because they're running legacy infrastructure.
  • Your employee commute emissions are 50% higher because most employees drive alone (you assumed public transit rates).
  • Your cloud provider's Scope 2 emissions are actually zero (they use 100% renewable energy), but their Scope 3 (hardware manufacturing) is massive.

Your actual Scope 3? 140,000 tCO2e. More than double what you reported.

Now imagine you set a Net Zero target based on the old number. Your entire decarbonization roadmap is wrong. Your SBTi submission is wrong. Your investor commitments are wrong.

The Greenwashing Risk

Here's the darker side: some companies deliberately use spend-based factors to look greener.

Example: A fashion brand sources garments from factories in Bangladesh. The actual factories use diesel generators (high emissions). But the spend-based factor for "textile manufacturing in South Asia" is an industry average that includes modern, efficient factories. Result? Their reported emissions are 30-40% lower than reality.

Is it deliberate? Maybe not. But it's convenient. And as regulators and auditors get smarter, this won't fly. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CS3D) and similar regulations are coming. They require traceability—not estimates.

What Good Scope 3 Reporting Looks Like

The companies getting this right are doing three things:

1. Supplier engagement at scale. They're not just asking for data—they're making it easy. Simple questionnaires, guided templates, support for small suppliers who don't have sustainability teams.

2. Hybrid methodology. Primary data for top 80% of spend (by emissions impact). Spend-based factors only for the long tail of low-impact suppliers. This balances accuracy with practicality.

3. Technology to manage complexity. You can't do this in Excel. Good Scope 3 programs use platforms that:

  • Integrate with procurement systems to identify high-impact suppliers
  • Send automated requests with guided questionnaires
  • Validate supplier data against benchmarks (is this number realistic?)
  • Track response rates and follow up automatically
  • Calculate emissions using both primary and secondary data

The Bottom Line

If you're still using spend-based factors for most of your Scope 3, you're not measuring your actual footprint. You're measuring an industry average that may or may not resemble your reality.

And in 2026, with BRSR Core assurance, CSRD traceability requirements, and increasing customer scrutiny, that's no longer acceptable.

The good news? Engaging suppliers doesn't have to be painful. With the right approach and the right tools, you can get 60-70% response rates and high-quality data in 4-6 months.
